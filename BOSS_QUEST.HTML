00. Philosophy

Core Principle: Laziness is a feature, not a bug. The system exploits laziness instead of fighting it. The time between receiving a notification and actually acting on it gives agents time to finish their work.

This document describes Quest Mode — a dopamine-engineered, self-motivating, cinematic quest system that turns post-completion grind into an addictive voluntary experience. It is not a task manager. It is not a gamification layer. It is a behavioral architecture that restructures the relationship between a human and their own unfinished work.

Traditional

"YOU FAILED YOUR TASK"

Pressure → Resistance → Avoidance

Motivation = obligation

8 tasks = 8 chores

Quality: MEH

Quest Mode

"yo psst... wanna see it evolve?"

Curiosity → Engagement → Momentum

Motivation = identity + tiny victories

8 tasks = 8 boss fights

Quality: XEX

Key Insight: Gamification fails when it feels corporate, but becomes addictive when it feels like lore. The system preserves the human ego, the fun, and the feeling of being creative instead of managed. The moment the client senses a template, the spell breaks. Every piece of content must feel hand-crafted for their specific quest.

The Breakthrough Formula

Laziness + Curiosity + 1 Click > Discipline + 8 Clicks + Boredom

The Compound Effect

Slower by calendar, faster by reality. "8 polished PRs in 3 days" beats "0 PRs in 2 weeks" every time. Each quest gets a dedicated polishing pass, so quality compounds — the second quest benefits from the first, the third from both previous. Motivation grows instead of depleting because each step produces a tangible, celebrated win. The system turns the natural human tendency to procrastinate into the very mechanism that ensures quality.

01. The Problem

What Works

Burst launch — the client (Lera, nail artist, whoever) fires off N agents from a Boss plan. All tasks start executing in parallel. The creative/fun part works great. The dopamine of launching something new carries the client through the initiation phase effortlessly.

What Breaks

Post-completion. Tasks are done, code is written, but PRs aren't created. Days pass. The client finds it boring, doesn't realize what they built (task names in English, client speaks Russian), and the whole redesign just... sits there. The creative high of the launch is gone, replaced by the drudgery of closing things out. This is the exact moment where traditional project management fails and Quest Mode takes over.

The Real Issue: Going back to a bot and clicking "Create PR" 8 times is not interesting. Especially when the task name is not in your language and you barely noticed what you overhauled. The system must bridge the gap between "I built something cool" and "I need to close it out" — and that bridge must feel like a victory march, not a death march.

Intended Emotional Transformation

"8 tasks..." ⟹ "ah fuck it" ⟹ *keeps playing*

MUST BECOME:

"WTF?" ⟹ "wow" ⟹ "omg key" ⟹ *pasted* ⟹ "Bitch! haha!" ⟹ PR created ⟹ *fucks off with big balls* ⟹ *new notification* ⟹ cycle repeats

02. Architecture

3-Stage Pipeline

Stage 1: Game Loading — Intelligence Gathering

The only stage that requires a human. Take a screenshot of "now", collect business type, vibe, colors, pain points. Pack it into a structured context. This is the seed that everything else grows from — the more specific and emotionally rich the input, the more resonant the quest chain output.

Status: Already works. We have a gold example of tenant seed. Redesign picture generation is straightforward.

Stage 2: Start Game — Vision Generation

Agent takes the screenshot + profile, generates a redesigned image (9:16, new palette, new structure). Gives everything to Boss (Codex in plan mode). Human sees the result and says "go" or "change X". The visual hook is the first motivator — seeing what your thing COULD look like creates the desire that carries through the entire quest chain.

Stage 3: Boss Mode — Quest Orchestration

This is where the magic happens. Boss does three things in one shot:

3a. Decomposition Analyze the difference between "before" and "after" → generate a list of concrete tasks (not abstract — by component: header, footer, navigation, cards, animations, etc.) 3b. SupaPlan Task Injection For each task, create a SupaPlan task with UUID. Inside each task — special sauce: - Specific polishing instructions (not "make it pretty" but "remove gap between X and Y, add transition, check 375px width") - Reference to redesign picture (which part is relevant) - Completeness criteria (so the agent knows when to stop) 3c. Quest Chain Link tasks in a chain by priority. Each task knows the UUID of the next. → Immediately fire first notification to client

05. Timing & Laziness

The delay between notification and action is not a bug — it's the feature. The laziness gap is where the agents do their work. If the client acted instantly, they'd find incomplete code. The laziness ensures that by the time they show up, everything is ready for their signature.

Timeline

T+0: Boss creates plan + N SupaPlan tasks ↓ T+0: First notification to client: "Mission 1 ready, here's cheatcode" ↓ Client: "Wait... I'm in a match/game..." ↓ T+5min: Task 1 already completed by agent (while client hesitates) ↓ T+10min: Task 2 completed ↓ T+15min: Task 3 completed ↓ Client: "Fine, let's see..." ↓ Opens chat, inserts cheatcode ↓ Task is ALREADY DONE. Agent already wrote the code. ↓ Cheatcode gives polish instructions → agent polishes ↓ PR with "⚡:" → auto-merge ↓ Notification Part 2: MISSION COMPLETE + spoiler + next cheatcode ↓ Cycle repeats

The client doesn't even know that while they were "being lazy", everything was already ready. They think they're the boss who comes in and says "polish it." In reality, they just press buttons in the right order — but the emotional experience is one of agency and triumph, not of being managed.

Auto-Merge

PR titles starting with ⚡: trigger a GitHub workflow that auto-merges. This exists not to eliminate PR creation, but to eliminate WAITING after creation. Create it, sign it, close the tab. GitHub handles the rest. The reward is instant — no CI pending, no review queue, no nothing. Click. Done. Glory.

Minimum friction. Maximum reward.

Two micro-dopamines instead of one macro-obligation. The first notification triggers the "WTF I built THAT?!" reflex. The second delivers the "MISSION COMPLETE" triumph. Between them, the polishing work happens — invisible, automatic, magical. The client experiences two emotional peaks per quest instead of one flat obligation.

Part 1: The Hook (WTF Stage)

Makes the client realize what they already built. Specific, in their language, not jargon. The key word is specific — not "a component was improved" but "the header was reborn as a cyber-panel with logo left, section tabs center, compact profile." This specificity is what triggers the "WTF" moment. Then hints at imperfections to spark curiosity. Provides the cheatcode as the key to the next level.

Part 2: The Victory (Finish the Bitch Stage)

Celebrates the polish. Gives achievement, drops a "skin" (UI improvement as unlockable). Nudges to create PR as the final triumph. Then — and this is critical — immediately provides a teaser about the next mission + its cheatcode. The loop never breaks. There is always one more door to open, one more boss to fight, one more achievement to unlock. The chain is the addiction.

09. Failure Modes & Recovery

Any system that depends on human behavior must account for failure modes. Quest Mode is no exception — the architecture is resilient precisely because it anticipates these specific failure points and has recovery strategies built in. Every failure mode has been observed in the wild during the first run.

Client doesn't forward Boss init message
Wall of text looks intimidating. Client opens it, says "что за ужас!", closes it.
Recovery: Use a cinematic comic trailer as the carrier. The visual hook bypasses the "too much text" reflex. Lera shared the comic with her Overwatch teammate — social proof collapsed the cringe barrier. Comics first, message second.
Notification feels like spam
Part 1 is too long, too generic, or arrives at a bad time.
Recovery: Make Part 1 shorter and punchier. The "WTF?" hook must land in under 3 seconds of reading. Front-load the specific achievement and the cool thing they built. If the first 10 words don't hit, the rest doesn't matter.
Achievements / skins feel repetitive
Boss templates notifications instead of crafting unique ones per quest.
Recovery: The UNIQUENESS MANDATE at the top of the Boss init message. Added explicit instruction: before writing each notification, check all previous ones. If any achievement/skin/spoiler repeats, rewrite. This is the #1 bug — enforce ruthlessly.
Client inserts cheatcode too early
Client is excited and pastes the UUID before the agent has finished the initial build.
Recovery: Agent gracefully handles this: "Task still cooking. The build is in progress — come back in ~5 minutes and paste again. The cheatcode never expires." No error, no frustration — just a gentle "not yet, but soon."
Client abandons mid-chain
Life happens. They do a few quests then disappear for days.
Recovery: The system is inherently patient. Notifications sit in Telegram. No deadlines, no pressure. When the client returns, the cheatcode still works. The SupaPlan task still has the polish instructions. Nothing expires. The fuse is lit, but the burn rate is the client's.
Auto-merge workflow breaks
CI fails, merge conflict, or the ⚡: prefix isn't recognized.
Recovery: The PR still exists. It just needs manual merge. The notification doesn't lie — the work is done. The auto-merge is convenience, not necessity. Worst case: one extra click. Still better than the old system.
Notification shows raw \n instead of line breaks
Boss writes notification text with escaped \n in JSON metadata. The notification skill sends the raw string to Telegram API without parsing escape sequences.
Recovery: Two-layer fix: (1) Boss Init Message now explicitly instructs "use real line breaks, not escaped \n" with WRONG/RIGHT examples. (2) Notification skill must parse \n → actual newlines before calling Telegram API. Belt and suspenders — fix it at generation time AND at send time.
Design Philosophy: The system is anti-fragile by design. Every failure mode has a graceful degradation path. The client never sees an error — only variations of "not yet" or "still here when you're ready." The fuse burns at the client's pace, not the system's.

Lessons Learned

  • "Boss didn't fail, Lera was lazy" — the system must assume the client won't act immediately and design around that. The laziness gap is the feature.
  • Comics as carriers work — Lera shared with Overwatch teammate = social proof = cringe barrier collapsed. Visual > text for initial engagement.
  • "Что за ужас!)" = she looked at it = win. Mockery is engagement. The first reaction doesn't have to be positive — it just has to be strong.
  • Providence, not coincidence — first failed attempt gave time to polish the fuse. The delay made the second attempt stronger.
  • Uniqueness is the entire game. One templated notification doesn't just fail that quest — it retroactively makes all previous unique ones feel like they were also templates. Trust is fragile.
  • Literal \n bug: Boss writes notification text with escaped newlines, and the notification skill sends them as-is. The client sees "...активирована.\n\nТы уже..." instead of proper paragraphs. The fix must happen at the notification sending layer — parse \n → real newlines before the Telegram API call.
  • CSV Autopsy (v2.1): Exported all 8 SupaPlan tasks from Supabase. Confirmed 100% template infection — every single notification_part1 uses identical body text ("ключевой блок интерфейса собран и выглядит как кадр из кибер-трейлера"), every single achievement is "Сигнал Из Матрицы", every skin is "Carbon Gold Precision", every spoiler is "следующий апгрейд двигает весь экран и заставляет контент жить в глубину". Even polish_instructions share identical edge checks and reference mapping across all 8 tasks. Only the Focus line and quest_name differ. Full overhaul needed.
